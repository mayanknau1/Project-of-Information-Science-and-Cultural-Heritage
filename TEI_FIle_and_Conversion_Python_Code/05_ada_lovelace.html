Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace (née Byron; 10 December 1815 – 27 November 1852), also known as Ada Lovelace, was an English mathematician and writer chiefly known for work on Charles Babbage's proposed mechanical general-purpose computer, the Analytical Engine. She was the first to recognise the machine had applications beyond pure calculation. Lovelace is often considered the first computer programmer.

Parentage and early life

Lovelace was the only legitimate child of poet Lord Byron and reformer Anne Isabella Milbanke. Lord Byron separated from his wife a month after Ada was born, and died when she was eight. Her mother promoted Ada's interest in mathematics and logic, in an effort to prevent her from developing her father's perceived insanity. Ada herself described her approach as "poetical science".

Education

From 1832, when she was seventeen, her mathematical abilities began to emerge. Her educators included Mary Somerville, the noted Scottish astronomer and mathematician, and, from 1840, the mathematician and logician Augustus De Morgan, who extended her much help in her mathematical studies, including study of advanced calculus topics and the numbers of Bernoulli.

Meeting Charles Babbage

Lovelace first met Charles Babbage on 5 June 1833, when she and her mother attended one of his Saturday night soirées with their mutual friend, and Lovelace's private tutor, Mary Somerville. Later that month Babbage invited Lovelace to see the prototype of his Difference Engine; she became fascinated with the machine. Her mathematical talents led her to a long working relationship and friendship with Babbage, and she was particularly interested in his work on the Analytical Engine.

Marriage

On 8 July 1835, Ada married William, 8th Baron King, becoming Lady King. In 1838 her husband was made Earl of Lovelace, and she thereby became Countess of Lovelace.

Social circle

Lovelace moved in distinguished scientific and literary circles: her acquaintances included the scientist Michael Faraday and the author Charles Dickens.

The Menabrea translation and the Notes

In 1840, Babbage was invited to give a seminar at the University of Turin in Turin about his Analytical Engine. Luigi Menabrea, a young Italian engineer and the future Prime Minister of Italy, transcribed Babbage's lecture into French (Notions sur la machine analytique de M. Charles Babbage, 1842). Babbage's friend Charles Wheatstone commissioned Lovelace to translate Menabrea's paper into English.

During a nine-month period in 1842–43, Lovelace translated Menabrea's article. She augmented the paper with seven notes, A to G, about three times longer than the translation. The translation and notes were then published in the September 1843 edition of Taylor's Scientific Memoirs under her initials AAL.

Note G and the first algorithm

The notes are important in the early history of computers, especially since Note G described, in complete detail, a method for calculating a sequence of Bernoulli numbers using the Analytical Engine, which might have run correctly had it ever been built. Based on this work, Lovelace is now widely considered the first computer programmer, and her method has been called the world's first published computer program.

Death and burial

Lovelace died at the age of 36 on 27 November 1852 from cervical cancer at her home in London. In August 1852, during her final illness, Charles Dickens visited her at her request and read to her a scene from his novel Dombey and Son. She was buried, at her request, next to her father at the Church of St Mary Magdalene in Hucknall, Nottinghamshire.

Legacy

Lovelace's work has been celebrated as a foundation of computer science. In 1980, the United States Department of Defense named a newly developed programming language Ada after her.
